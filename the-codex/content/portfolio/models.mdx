# Models

Famous portfolios: All-Weather, 60/40, Swensen. Pre-built allocation frameworks offer tested starting points for constructing a portfolio. This page surveys the most influential models, their design rationale, and historical performance. It also covers the core investment strategies and approaches used to implement any portfolio.

## Investment Strategies

### Buy & Hold

Invest capital as soon as it is available, then hold indefinitely without selling.

**Pros:**
- Maximises time in the market
- Eliminates transaction costs and capital gains events from rebalancing
- Simplest to execute

**Cons:**
- Portfolio weights drift over time as assets appreciate at different rates
- May feel psychologically difficult for beginners during large drawdowns

### Strategic Rebalancing

Buy & Hold plus periodic rebalancing (typically every 6 months to 2 years) back to target weights.

**Cons:**
- Each rebalancing event triggers capital gains taxes
- Incurs transaction costs

### Dollar Cost Averaging (DCA)

Invest a fixed amount at regular intervals (e.g., €500/month), regardless of market conditions.

- Reduces the impact of entry-point timing and volatility relative to a lump-sum Buy & Hold
- Particularly useful when accumulating capital incrementally from income
- Studies consistently show that systematic monthly investment outperforms attempts to time purchases around market dips

### Value Averaging (VA)

The portfolio must grow by a fixed target amount each period. If the portfolio grew less than the target (e.g., because prices fell), invest more; if it grew more, invest less or hold cash.

**Compared to DCA:**
- Buys more when prices are low and less when they are high — mechanically contrarian
- Risk: during prolonged bull markets, VA accumulates a large cash reserve that misses compounding
- Harder to track and execute consistently
- Appropriate only for experienced investors comfortable with the liquidity management required

## Investing Approaches

### Passive vs. Active

| | Passive | Active |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Core instruments | ETFs and index funds | ETFs, index funds, and individual stocks |
| Stock selection | No individual stock picking | Yes — selecting specific securities |
| Goal | Match the market return | Beat the market return |
| Cost | Low TER | Higher fees, taxes from trading |

Passive investors can still make strategic choices about **asset allocation** (which markets, which sectors) without selecting individual securities. Most evidence shows active management does not consistently outperform passive indexing over long periods, after fees.

### Activity Level Spectrum

You can choose how active to be along four dimensions:

1. **Asset Allocation** — decide what proportion goes into equities, bonds, commodities, etc.
2. **Security Selection** — pick individual ETFs or stocks within each class
3. **Rebalancing** — restore target weights when drift occurs
4. **Tactical Asset Allocation (TAA)** — temporarily override strategic weights based on market conditions

**Tactical Asset Allocation** is an active approach: you adjust sector or asset class weights based on your current market view. For example, after a market crash you might increase the weighting of defensive sectors (Consumer Staples, Healthcare) expecting a rebound. This is fundamentally different from mechanical rebalancing — it requires a correct market prediction and is therefore significantly riskier.

### Leverage Investing

Leverage amplifies both gains and losses by using borrowed capital. A ×2 leveraged position doubles the potential return but also doubles the potential loss (and adds the cost of the loan).

Leveraged ETFs exist (e.g., ×2 or ×3 S&P 500), but three structural factors erode their expected returns significantly:

1. **Volatility drag** — due to the mathematics of compounding, a fund with ×2 volatility does not produce ×2 long-term returns. Volatility itself destroys the expected multiplier.
2. **Financing cost** — the borrowing rate (often SOFR-linked) is charged daily. Even at moderate rates, this erodes compounding over years.
3. **Higher TER** — leveraged ETFs carry expense ratios above 0.5% compared to sub-0.1% for standard index ETFs.

Net result: real expected returns from leveraged ETFs are substantially below the naive multiplier, while volatility is doubled.

Leverage only makes sense if: you fully understand the risks, you have a very long investment horizon, and you are deliberately choosing to invest on margin.

## Famous Portfolio Models

### The 60/40 Portfolio

60% global equities + 40% government bonds. The most widely cited baseline for balanced long-term investing. Equities drive growth; bonds cushion drawdowns. The proportions can be adjusted along the `100 - Age` heuristic as the investor ages.

### Ray Dalio's All Seasons Portfolio

Designed to perform across any economic environment — growth, recession, inflation, and deflation. Target allocation:

- 30% equities
- 40% long-term bonds
- 15% intermediate bonds
- 7.5% gold
- 7.5% commodities

Very stable and consistent across market cycles, at the cost of lower long-term returns compared to equity-heavy portfolios. Suitable for risk-averse investors or those nearing retirement. TAA adjustments (e.g., temporarily increasing equities when young) can improve returns without fully abandoning the risk-parity structure.

Resources:
- [portfoliocharts.com — All Seasons](https://portfoliocharts.com/portfolios/all-seasons-portfolio/)
- [lazyportfolioetf.com — Ray Dalio All Weather](https://www.lazyportfolioetf.com/allocation/ray-dalio-all-weather/)

### US Sector Portfolio (XLK / XLV / XLP / XLY)

A US-focused equity portfolio distributed across four non-correlated GICS sectors:

- **XLK** — Technology
- **XLV** — Healthcare
- **XLP** — Consumer Staples
- **XLY** — Consumer Discretionary

This combination provides exposure to both cyclical and defensive sectors, reducing sector concentration without moving outside US equities.

### David Swensen's Yale Endowment Model

Swensen diversified aggressively beyond traditional stocks and bonds:

- Domestic equities (~30%)
- Foreign developed equities (~15%)
- Emerging market equities (~10%)
- Real estate / REITs (~20%)
- US Treasury bonds (~15%)
- TIPS (~10%)

Designed for very long-horizon institutional capital. The high real estate and alternative allocations are difficult to replicate exactly for retail investors.

### The Permanent Portfolio (Harry Browne)

25% equities, 25% long-term bonds, 25% gold, 25% cash. Designed to perform in all four economic conditions: prosperity, recession, inflation, and deflation. Simpler than All Seasons with broadly similar resilience characteristics.
