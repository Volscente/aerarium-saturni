# Diversification

Risk management, correlation, and the "Free Lunch." Diversification is the only strategy in finance that reduces risk without sacrificing expected return. This page explains why uncorrelated assets combine to produce a more efficient portfolio.

## Rationale

Concentrating a portfolio in a single asset exposes it to that asset's full idiosyncratic risk. Diversification reduces this by spreading exposure across assets whose returns do not move in lockstep.

Think of **asset allocation** as dividing your portfolio into distinct buckets with different time horizons and objectives:

- **Equities** → long-term wealth accumulation (10+ years)
- **Bonds** → medium-term capital preservation and income (1-10 years)

## Systematic vs. Unsystematic Risk

- **Unsystematic risk** (company or sector risk) — can be eliminated through diversification
- **Systematic risk** (market risk) — affects all assets and cannot be diversified away; it is compensated by the equity risk premium

### Beta

**Beta** is the standard measure of systematic risk. It quantifies a security's sensitivity relative to the overall market:

- **Beta = 1** — the security moves in line with the market
- **Beta = 2** — the security is twice as volatile as the market
- **Beta < 1** — the security is less volatile than the market

Limitations of Beta as a risk measure:

- There is no robust empirical evidence that higher beta reliably produces higher returns over the long run — the theoretical relationship between beta and expected return has not held consistently across periods
- Beta is estimated from historical data and is therefore unstable — the same security can show materially different beta values depending on the measurement period and market benchmark chosen
- Because it is impossible to observe the entire investable market, all beta estimates are approximations subject to benchmark choice

## Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up Approach

| Approach | Description |
| --- | --- |
| **Bottom-Up** | Select a security you believe in, regardless of sector, P/E, or beta |
| **Top-Down** | Start from a macro view of the economy, identify sectors likely to benefit, then find the ETF or index tracking that sector |

The top-down approach is more consistent with passive investing. Rather than picking stocks, you identify the structural forces at play and gain exposure through index-level instruments.

## Allocation Rules

### Bonds vs. Equities

Two widely used heuristics to determine the bond allocation as a percentage of the portfolio:

- `100 − Age` (e.g., age 30 → 30% bonds, 70% equities)
- `(Age − 40) × 2` (more conservative; produces 0% bonds at age 40)

Neither is prescriptive — adjust for risk tolerance, income stability, and investment horizon.

### Concentration Limits

| Rule | Limit |
| --- | --- |
| Single security (individual stock or bond) | ≤ 5% of total portfolio |
| Single sector | ≤ 20% |
| Commodities | ≤ 10% |
| International / non-domestic exposure | ≥ 20% |
| ETF Expense Ratio | ≤ 0.5% |
| Bond credit rating | Minimum "A" — never hold junk bonds |

- Prefer 80/20 (equities/bonds) over 100% equities: comparable long-term returns with materially lower volatility
- Balance between Value and Growth investment approaches
- Balance between Short-Term Bond ETFs (e.g., BND) and Long-Term Bond ETFs (e.g., TLT)
- Include Emerging Market exposure (approximately 10-15%, large-cap focus)

## GICS Sectors

The Global Industry Classification Standard (GICS) divides the equity market into 11 sectors. Each behaves differently depending on the phase of the economic cycle.

| Sector | Stability | Market Phase | ETFs (US) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Consumer Staples | Stable | Late, Recession | XLP, FSTA |
| Healthcare | Stable | Late, Recession | XLV, FHLC |
| Utilities | Stable | Late, Recession | XLU, FUTY |
| Energy | Volatile; sensitive to geopolitics | Late | XLE, FENY |
| Financials | High volatility; interest rate sensitive | Early | XLF, FNCL |
| Real Estate | Stable but underperformed over 20yr | Early | XLRE, SCHH |
| Consumer Discretionary | Cyclical | Early | XLY, FDIS |
| Technology | Sensitive to economic health | Early, Mid | XLK, FTEC |
| Industrials | Sensitive to housing and GDP | Early | XLI, FIDU |
| Materials | Sensitive to housing and GDP | Early, Late | XLB, FMAT |
| Communication Services | Sensitive to economic health | Mid | XLC |

Market cycle phases:
- **Early** — growth/recovery phase
- **Mid** — consolidation
- **Late** — stagnation
- **Recession** — contraction

A globally diversified portfolio naturally contains all sectors, smoothing out the cyclical swings. Reference: [finviz.com/map](https://finviz.com/map.ashx) for a real-time visual breakdown of US sector weights.

## Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)

Markets are efficient: they react only to new information. All publicly available information — P/E ratios, beta values, growth forecasts — is already reflected in current prices.

Implications:
- Consistently identifying mispriced securities before the market corrects them is extremely rare
- The potential growth of any company is already embedded in its share price
- Only genuinely new information can shift prices; known information cannot

Traders attempt to find market inefficiencies (underpriced securities before the market discovers them). This is difficult and the basis for why passive investing outperforms active management over long horizons for most investors.

## Behavioural Principles

- Do not trade individual securities frequently — a minimum horizon of 10 years is appropriate for equities
- Do your own research; external recommendations are biased
- Rebalance every 3 years (or when portfolio weights drift materially), shifting allocations toward more conservative instruments as you age
- Accept that the market will fall — the portfolio will recover if it is properly diversified and the time horizon is long enough
