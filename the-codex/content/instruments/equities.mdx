# Equities

Stocks, ownership rights, and dividend basics. Equities represent fractional ownership of a company, entitling shareholders to residual earnings and voting rights. This page covers how equity markets function and how stocks are valued.

## Market Capitalisation

Market capitalisation is the total market value of a company's outstanding shares (share price × number of shares). It is used to classify companies by size:

![Market capitalisation categories by total value in USD billions](/instruments/market-cap-categories.png)

| Category | Market Cap (USD) |
| --- | --- |
| Mega-cap | ≥ $200B |
| Large-cap | $10B - $200B |
| Mid-cap | $2B - $10B |
| Small-cap | $250M - $2B |
| Micro-cap | < $250M |

## Financial Statements

Companies are legally required to publish financial statements on a quarterly basis. Three core documents exist:

- **Balance Sheet** — reports the company's Assets, Liabilities, and Equity at a point in time
  - Assets = Liabilities + Equity
- **Income Statement** — discloses Revenues, Expenses, and Net Profit over a period
  - Net Profit = Revenues − Expenses
- **Cash Flow Statement** — tracks cash moving in and out of the business, split across three activities:
  1. **Core Operations** — cash generated by the business itself
  2. **Investing** — capital expenditure, acquisitions, asset sales
  3. **Financing** — debt issuance, equity raises, dividends paid (shows where money is coming from)

## Earnings

### Definitions

- **Earnings (E)** — company profits for a period (Revenues − Expenses)
- **Earnings per Share (EPS)** — Earnings ÷ shares outstanding; the portion of total earnings attributable to a single share
- **Price-to-Earnings (P/E)** — share price ÷ EPS; how much investors pay for each dollar of earnings. Used to assess whether a company is overvalued or undervalued relative to its peers

### Example

| Metric | Company A | Company B | Note |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Earnings | $1M | $2M | |
| Shares Outstanding | 200K | 1M | |
| EPS | $5 (1M ÷ 200K) | $2 (2M ÷ 1M) | Company A allocates more earnings per share |
| Share Price | $500 | $40 | Price alone does not determine value — you can buy more shares of a cheaper stock |
| P/E | 100 (500 ÷ 5) | 20 (40 ÷ 2) | Company B is cheaper relative to earnings: $1 of earnings costs $20 vs. $100 |

### How to Read an Earnings Release

Earnings reports include an **estimated** value (analyst consensus) and the **actual** reported value. The difference is called the **Surprise**.

![Earnings release showing estimated vs. actual revenue and the surprise percentage](/instruments/earnings-reading.png)

In the example above, estimated revenue was $148.94B and the actual came in at $152.3B — a positive surprise.

## Book Value

- **Book Value** — the net value of the company if it pays off all its debts and liquidates its assets (Assets − Liabilities)
- **Book Value per Share (BVPS)** — Book Value ÷ shares outstanding
- **Price-to-Book (P/B)** — share price ÷ BVPS; how much the market pays relative to the company's accounting value

## Growth vs. Value

Companies are often classified along a growth-value spectrum:

| | Growth Companies | Value Companies |
| --- | --- | --- |
| P/E and P/B | High | Low |
| Dividends | Rarely paid | Regularly paid |
| Investor profile | Bets on future earnings expansion | Seeks underpriced assets with current cash returns |

- Short-term price predictions (e.g. 1-year forecasts) are priced into share prices as "Future Price" estimates, but they are unreliable guides for decision-making — a 20-25 year horizon is more appropriate for most investors

## Beta

**Beta** measures the sensitivity of a security's returns relative to the overall market. A beta of 1 means the security moves in line with the market; a beta above 1 means it amplifies market moves (higher risk and higher potential return); a beta below 1 means it is less volatile than the market.

Beta is used in the **Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)** to estimate the expected return of an asset given its systematic risk. Higher beta implies higher required return to compensate for additional volatility.

## Ticker

A **ticker** (or ticker symbol) is the unique identifier for a financial instrument — stock, ETF, index, or bond — on an exchange. For example, `SPY` is the ticker for the SPDR S&P 500 ETF.

## Dividends

### Definition

A **dividend** is a distribution of profits by a corporation to its shareholders. Any surplus not paid out is reinvested in the company by the Board of Directors to generate future value.

Key dates:

- **Declaration Date** — the date on which the dividend is announced and the amount confirmed
- **Record Date** — the date used to identify which shareholders are entitled to receive the dividend
- **Ex-Dividend Date** — shares must be purchased *before* this date to qualify for the upcoming dividend payment

### Dividend Yield vs. Dividend Rate

- **Dividend Yield** — the ratio of the annual dividend to the current share price. In rising markets, share prices increase faster than dividends, compressing the yield. A falling dividend yield does not necessarily mean lower dividends — it may reflect share price appreciation.
- **Dividend Rate** — the absolute annual dividend per share. Companies have no legal obligation to pay dividends or to maintain a consistent frequency.

### Distributing vs. Accumulating ETFs

For ETFs, the distinction between distributing and accumulating matters primarily for tax purposes. Mechanically, even a distributing ETF does not hold dividend cash in the fund — it liquidates a portion of its holdings to fund the payout.

This means a distributing ETF and an accumulating ETF in which you manually sell an equivalent slice are economically identical before taxes. The difference is that accumulating ETFs defer the tax event, which benefits long-term compounding (the _Steuerstundeffekt_).

High-dividend-yield ETFs carry a similar logic: the yield is not free money extracted from somewhere other than the fund's own NAV. Seeking high yield in an ETF does not produce better total returns than holding an accumulating equivalent.
