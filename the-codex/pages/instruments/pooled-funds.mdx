# Pooled Funds

ETFs, Mutual Funds, and Index Funds. Pooled investment vehicles allow investors to gain diversified exposure to a basket of securities through a single instrument. This page compares the main structures and examines their cost, tax, and liquidity implications.

## Mutual Funds

### Definition

A mutual fund pools money from many investors to purchase a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, or other securities. A professional manager oversees the pool and decides what to buy and sell. Hedge funds operate similarly but also take short positions — betting on securities that are expected to fall in price.

### Characteristics

- Active management means they generally cannot beat the index they are benchmarked against, yet they do not aim to replicate it — they aim to beat it
- Higher fees than index funds, up to 5%
- Frequent trading of underlying securities results in higher tax obligations for investors
- Subject to **survivorship bias**: underperforming funds are routinely merged into better-performing ones, erasing their records. This inflates the apparent average performance of the surviving fund universe and makes the overall track record of active management look better than it actually is

### Types

| Type | Mechanics |
| --- | --- |
| Open-End | New investor capital flows directly into the fund |
| Closed-End | Like an ETF — the fund issues a fixed number of shares that trade on an exchange; you cannot invest directly in the fund itself |

## Index Funds

### Definition

An index fund tracks a specified basket of underlying market securities. The goal is to replicate the composition and performance of a market index — not to outperform it.

- **S&P 500** — 500 companies representing the US stock market
- **CAC 40** — 40 companies representing the French market
- **DAX** — Germany
- **Nikkei** — Japan
- **TSX** — Canada

### Characteristics

- Low fees (Expense Ratio typically up to 0.09%)
- No active management decisions — the fund just holds the index constituents

### Price-Weighted vs. Market-Cap Weighted

- **Dow Jones** is price-weighted: shares with a higher price exert more influence on the index
- **S&P 500** is market-cap weighted: each company's influence reflects its size relative to the others — a more accurate representation of economic importance

## Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs)

### Definition

An ETF is a fund whose shares trade on a stock exchange, just like individual stocks. Unlike an index fund (where you invest money directly into the fund), an ETF issues shares that track an underlying index or basket of assets.

- **Index Fund** → you invest money into the fund pool
- **ETF** → you buy shares of a fund that tracks the index (e.g., SPY tracks the S&P 500, XLF tracks the financial sector)

The performance difference between an index fund and its ETF equivalent is typically explained by the ETF's fees.

### Advantages

| Advantage | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Passive | Replicates an index without trying to outperform it |
| Tradable | Can be bought and sold at any point during market hours |
| Economic | Low fees compared to active funds |

### Errors to Avoid

- Using ETFs as a vehicle for active trading — this defeats the purpose
- Switching from one ETF to a cheaper equivalent: although the new ETF has a lower TER, selling triggers a taxable event and the savings rarely compensate
- Investing in thematic ETFs without deep understanding of the sector — this is factor investing, not passive investing
- Ignoring currency risk; currency hedging is expensive and usually not worth it

### Accumulating vs. Distributing

Accumulating (Acc) share classes reinvest dividends automatically rather than paying them out. This is generally preferable for long-term investors:

- More capital compounds over 30 years — the tax deferral effect (_Steuerstundeffekt_)
- The inflation effect reduces the real value of future tax payments: €10 of tax today is worth much more than €10 in 30 years
- Note: even a distributing ETF does not hold dividends in its AUM — it liquidates a portion of the fund to pay them out. A distributing ETF and an accumulating ETF in which you sell an equivalent amount are mathematically equivalent, ignoring taxes.

### Comparing ETFs

To identify the most liquid ETFs on any given index:

1. Go to [tradingview.com/etf-screener](https://tradingview.com/etf-screener)
2. Add the **Average Volume (90 days)** column
3. Sort descending — higher average volume means greater liquidity and tighter bid-ask spreads

### How to Inspect an ETF

1. Find the ETF on the issuer's website (e.g., iShares, Vanguard) and open **Holdings**
2. Verify no single company represents more than 5–7% of the fund's weight
3. Check the Expense Ratio (TER)
4. Read the ETF factsheet and prospectus
5. Check the "Annual Report" and "Investor Relations" documents for the Risk section

### Evaluation Checklist

| Criterion | What to look for |
| --- | --- |
| TER / Expense Ratio | ≤ 0.2% is good; ≥ 0.7% is expensive |
| Fund domicile | Ireland or Luxembourg preferred for dividend withholding tax treaties |
| Index replication | Physical replication preferred over synthetic |
| AUM / Fund size | Larger is better — more liquidity and more accurate index tracking |
| Distributing vs. Accumulating | Prefer Acc for long-term |
| Hedging | Avoid currency-hedged versions — hedging is expensive |
| ETF age | At least 1–2 years of track record |
| Tracking error | Difference between ETF performance and underlying index; smaller is better |
| Benchmark comparison | Consistent outperformance of its own index may indicate imprecise replication and hidden risk |

Resources: [JustETF.com](https://justetf.com), [etfdb.com](https://etfdb.com), issuer websites.

Avoid thematic ETFs. They introduce sector concentration risk and undermine the passive investing thesis.

### Major ETF Issuers

### Smart Beta

Smart beta ETFs sit between passive and active investing. They weight holdings according to a specific **factor** rather than purely by market cap:

| Factor | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Value | Favours companies trading at low valuation ratios (low P/E, low P/B) |
| Low Volatility | Selects securities with historically low price fluctuation |
| Momentum | Selects companies with the strongest recent price appreciation |

The best-performing factor changes depending on the market cycle. Smart beta is not passive investing — it requires a view on which factor is currently favourable.

## Money Market Funds

Money market funds (_fondi monetari_) invest in short-duration, high-quality debt instruments. Their primary use is preserving capital while earning a return above a standard current account — commonly used for the emergency fund pillar of personal finance.

### Characteristics

- Low sensitivity to interest rate changes: short maturities limit price volatility
- Returns track the prevailing short-term rate (e.g., ESTR); when rates approach zero, returns approach zero
- In Italy, UCITS-compliant money market ETFs are taxed at 12.5% — more favourable than the standard 26% capital gains rate, depending on the fund's underlying composition

### XEON

XEON is the most widely referenced money market ETF in European retail markets. Key mechanics:

- It holds medium-to-long-term bonds as collateral to secure a swap rate, which it then pays to investors — the underlying portfolio is therefore decoupled from the index it tracks
- Minimal interest rate sensitivity in normal conditions, but if the rate curve collapses to zero the fund yields nothing — at that point a standard deposit account may be preferable
- Taxation in Italy is 12.5% due to its government bond-heavy collateral, but always verify the current fund composition as this can change

### Cost Awareness

Broker costs extend beyond transaction commissions. Before assuming a money market fund is cheap to hold, check for custody fees, inactivity fees, and any bid-ask spread applied by the broker.

### Low Rate Environments

When short-term rates are near zero, money market fund returns become negligible. Fixed-rate deposit accounts (_conti vincolati_) offering a guaranteed rate for a fixed term may then be worth considering as a partial hedge — accepting a liquidity trade-off in exchange for a locked-in yield. Returns remain very low in absolute terms; the complexity is rarely justified for small sums.

## CFDs (Contracts for Difference)

CFDs are derivative instruments that do not give you direct ownership of the underlying asset. They are built on top of a stock (or other security) rather than representing it. Avoid CFDs: they carry counterparty risk, introduce leverage implicitly, and are not appropriate for long-term investment.
