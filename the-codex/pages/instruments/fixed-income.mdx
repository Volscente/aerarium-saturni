# Fixed Income

Bonds, treasury bills, and interest rate sensitivity. Fixed-income instruments are debt contracts that pay a defined stream of cash flows. This page explains how bonds are priced, how interest rate changes affect them, and their role in capital preservation.

## Bond Anatomy

A bond is a debt instrument issued by a government or company to raise capital. The issuer promises to repay the principal at maturity and pay periodic interest (the coupon).

| Term | Definition |
| --- | --- |
| **Face Value / Par Value / Principal** | The amount returned to the investor at maturity (e.g., €1,000) |
| **Maturity Date** | The date on which the principal is repaid |
| **Coupon Rate** | The annual interest paid on the principal (e.g., 6% → €60/year) |
| **Price** | The current market price of the bond, which may differ from face value |
| **Yield to Maturity (YTM)** | The effective annual return if the bond is held to maturity; used in bond pricing |

## The Price–Yield Relationship

Bond prices and interest rates move in opposite directions. If you hold a bond paying 5% and market rates rise to 6%, your bond becomes less attractive — its market price falls to compensate. Conversely, when rates fall, existing bonds with higher coupons become more valuable.

Bond coupon rates at issuance are influenced by:
- The prevailing government interest rate
- The credit quality of the issuer

## Types

### By Issuer

| Type | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Government bonds | Lower default risk; tax treatment often more favourable |
| Corporate bonds | Higher coupon to compensate for greater credit risk |

### By Duration

- **Short-term**: 1–4 years — includes Italian _Buoni Ordinari del Tesoro_ (BOT), short-term government bills issued at a discount and repaid at par; the Italian equivalent of US Treasury Bills
- **Long-term**: 10–30 years

### Individual Bonds vs. Bond ETFs

| | Individual Bonds | Bond ETFs |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Principal return | Yes — at maturity | No — the fund has no maturity date |
| Dividend income | Fixed and predictable | Variable — the fund holds bonds of different maturities |
| Cost | High (typically €10,000+ minimum) | Low; accessible in small amounts |
| Diversification | Low — concentrated exposure | High — spread across many issuers |
| Recommended use | Large portfolios; specific maturity matching | General use |

## Risks

- **Default risk** — the issuer fails to repay principal or interest; corporate risk exceeds government risk
- **Credit quality deterioration** — a rating downgrade (e.g., AAA → AA) reduces the bond's market price
- **Interest rate risk** — rising rates reduce the value of existing fixed-rate bonds

## Duration

Duration is the time-weighted average of the present value of future cash flows. It measures how sensitive a bond's price is to changes in interest rates.

- Long-maturity bonds have higher duration and are therefore more sensitive to rate changes
- **Interpretation**: if a bond fund shows an Average Duration of 6.6 years, a 1% rise in rates decreases its value by approximately 6.6%; a 1% fall increases it by 6.6%

## Evaluation

- Credit ratings (Moody's, S&P) run from AAA (highest quality) downward. These ratings compare bonds against each other, not against the market in general
- Avoid **High Yield** bonds (also called Junk Bonds): these are issued by companies with very low credit scores. The label "High Yield" refers to the coupon required to attract investors, not to dividend yield — do not confuse the two
- Government bonds are generally preferred for safety
- Long-term bond ETFs (7–10 or 20+ years) can act as a counterweight during equity market downturns

## Advantages

- **Stability** — lower volatility than equities; acts as a safety cushion, particularly for investors close to or in retirement
- **Regular income** — predictable coupon payments
- **Portfolio diversification** — low correlation with equities in most market environments

## Bond ETFs

A bond ETF holds a basket of bonds and continuously replaces those approaching the lower bound of the fund's target maturity range (e.g., a "3–5 year" ETF sells any bond that falls below 2 years remaining).

The main drawback of bond ETFs is that their price is heavily influenced by interest rate changes rather than the bonds themselves. Because nobody can reliably predict rate movements, the future value of a bond ETF is uncertain.

### iBonds (iShares Target Maturity Bond ETFs)

iShares created iBonds to address the unpredictability of standard bond ETFs. An iBond fund:

- Holds bonds all maturing in the same target year
- Holds them to maturity, making it resistant to interest rate fluctuations — like owning an individual bond
- Provides diversification across many issuers — unlike owning a single bond
- Is delisted after the target maturity date; capital is reimbursed to investors
- Can be purchased after the initial launch date

Government iBonds are preferred due to more favourable tax treatment compared to corporate versions.

**Limitations vs. individual bonds:**
- A TER is charged (individual bonds have no management fee)
- iBonds cannot be used to offset capital losses (_minus-valenze_)
- Bonds within the fund that mature before the target date have their proceeds reinvested in very short-duration securities — these carry variable and typically lower returns
