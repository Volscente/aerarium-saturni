# Brokers

Comparison of brokers, custodians, and safety. The choice of broker determines the cost structure, asset safety, and available instruments for an investor. This page evaluates the key dimensions for selecting and trusting a custodian.

## Definition

A broker provides access to financial markets — allowing you to buy, sell, and hold investment instruments. Examples include Trade Republic, DEGIRO, and Interactive Brokers.

## Commission Fees

Commission structure determines the true cost of investing, especially for smaller portfolios. The same nominal fee represents very different percentage costs depending on investment size:

| Monthly investment | Commission | Effective cost |
| --- | --- | --- |
| €100 | €1 | 1.0% |
| €500 | €1 | 0.2% |
| €1,000 | €1 | 0.1% |

The relevant question is not how cheap the commission is in absolute terms, but what percentage of your investment it represents. For small regular purchases, zero-commission or subscription-model brokers (like Trade Republic) are significantly more efficient.

## Capital Insurance

Client assets at regulated brokers are typically insured or ring-fenced from the broker's own balance sheet. In the EU, investor compensation schemes (e.g., the German EdW or the European Investor Compensation Scheme) cover up to €20,000 per client in the event of broker insolvency. Check the specific limit for your country and broker.

## Account Types: OMNIBUS vs. Segregated

| Type | Description | Risk |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **OMNIBUS** | Your holdings are recorded only in the broker's internal database, not directly with the fund issuer (e.g., iShares) | If the broker fails, recovering assets is more complex; you are a creditor of the broker |
| **Segregated** | Your name is registered directly with the fund issuer; assets are legally held in your name | Safer in a broker insolvency; higher costs |

Most retail brokers use OMNIBUS accounts. Segregated accounts are available through some private banks and custodians at higher cost. For most investors, the regulatory protections of an OMNIBUS account at a well-regulated broker are sufficient.

**Practical implication**: a broker failure with OMNIBUS accounts does not automatically mean assets are lost, but recovery may take longer and be more complicated than with segregated accounts.
