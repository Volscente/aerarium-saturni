# FIRE Movement

Financial Independence, Retire Early (FIRE) is a framework for accumulating enough capital to live indefinitely off investment returns. The core idea is to build a portfolio large enough that a sustainable annual withdrawal covers all living expenses — permanently.

## Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Withdrawal Rate | Percentage of the total invested portfolio withdrawn each year during the FIRE period. Literature suggests 3–4%. |
| Saving Rate | (Income − Expenses) / Income |
| Retirement Spending | Total annual expenses during the retirement period. |

## Requirements

1. **Financial Awareness** — Know your financial situation, income, and ongoing expenses before planning any strategy.

## Process

The FIRE journey has two structural steps:

1. Drafting an **Investor Policy Statement** — a written commitment to your investment objectives and strategy
2. Executing a **Portfolio Shift** — gradually rebalancing toward lower volatility as you approach decumulation

---

## Investor Policy Statement

An Investor Policy Statement (IPS) is a document listing the major guidelines and objectives for your investment strategy. Writing it down forces clarity and prevents emotional decision-making during market turbulence.

It should include:

1. **Objectives** — Why are you investing?
   - Target age for reaching Financial Independence
   - Target percentage of Financial Independence (partial or full)
   - A plan for fighting inflation

2. **Overall Strategy** — Asset allocation, contribution schedule, rebalancing rules

### Goals

The IPS formalises what the FIRE movement is trying to achieve. Goals typically span from:

- **IAPA (Inflation-Adjusted Perpetual Annuity)** — A regular payment that adjusts with inflation indefinitely (e.g., €5,000/month in real terms)
- **Pension integration** — Supplementing the public pension to reach a target income
- **Early retirement** — Exiting employment before statutory pension age

The overarching objective is to satisfy "Retirement Spending" — reaching a portfolio size where you never need to grow it further.

## Portfolio Shift

### Motivation

A 100% equity portfolio that loses 20% in year 19 of a 20-year plan cannot recover in time. A single bad sequence of returns near the end of the accumulation phase is disproportionately destructive — which is why a gradual rebalancing toward lower volatility is essential.

### Objectives

- During the **accumulation phase**: maximise expected return
- During the **decumulation phase**: minimise the probability of running out of money

### Process

The goal is to slowly rebalance toward a lower-volatility allocation as the accumulation phase ends. Various formulas exist for computing the target equity share at each age — one common heuristic is:

> **Equity Share (%) = 132 − 1.5 × Age**

### Tips

1. Adjust the monthly contribution split rather than selling existing securities — avoids taxable events
2. Deploy one-off windfalls (work bonuses, large cash inflows) toward the target allocation
3. Never sell losing securities to rebalance — wait for recovery or redirect new contributions

## Withdrawal Rate

### The 4% Rule

This rule originates from William Bengen's 1994 research and was later revisited by three professors at Trinity University in 1998 — known as the Trinity Study. They found that a balanced portfolio (50% equities / 50% bonds) can sustain withdrawals of 4% per year for approximately 30 years with high probability.

Limitations of this research:

1. It is based on US market data from a specific historical period — its applicability to global portfolios or longer horizons is uncertain
2. The 30-year horizon was designed for traditional retirement, not decades-long early retirement

### The 3% Rule

More recent studies — also associated with the Trinity Study framework — suggest a more conservative 3% withdrawal rate, which extends the sustainable horizon and provides a larger margin of safety for those retiring early.

## Sequence of Returns Risk

### Definition

Sequence of Returns Risk is the danger that a bad sequence of early returns — not just the average return — can permanently damage a retirement portfolio. The order in which returns arrive matters as much as their magnitude.

### Impact

Suppose your portfolio reaches €1,000,000 at the end of the accumulation phase, but the final three years deliver −15%, −12%, and −13%. The terminal value is drastically lower than expected, and you begin withdrawing from a severely diminished base.

The same average return distributed differently — with losses at the beginning of the accumulation phase rather than the end — would produce a much better outcome. Bad returns early in accumulation are recoverable; bad returns late are not.

## Decumulation

### Definition

The financial lifecycle divides into an **accumulation phase** (building wealth) and a **decumulation phase** (drawing it down). In practice, the boundary is not always clean — some income may continue during decumulation. The distinction is conceptual rather than absolute.

The goal during decumulation is the **Inflation-Adjusted Perpetual Annuity (IAPA)**: an annual income that grows with inflation and never runs out.

### Rationale

Some investors ask whether they could skip the decumulation phase entirely by converting assets into recurring income streams — rental properties, bond coupons, dividends. This was the traditional approach, relying on government pension certainty that no longer exists for most people.

Critical problems with this approach today:

1. Statutory pension age may be 75 or higher by the time current workers retire
2. Public pension payouts are likely to be very low relative to pre-retirement income

### Critical Points

There are six variables that determine whether a decumulation plan is viable:

1. **Longevity Risk** — The decumulation phase may last 30–35 years. Plan for it.
2. **Retirement Spending** — Estimate annual expenses at retirement:
   - Start from current lifestyle costs
   - Apply an inflation adjustment
   - Include a buffer for random events (health, travel, family)
3. **Multiple Income Sources** — Public pension, rental income, BaV, ETF withdrawals — how many do you have, and are they inflation-adjusted?
4. **Generational Wealth** — How much, if any, do you want to leave behind? This affects the required portfolio size.
5. **Taxes** — Always compute in after-tax terms. Tax rules may change; plan conservatively.
6. **Expected Investment Returns** — Your withdrawal rate assumptions must align with realistic expected returns for your asset allocation.

### Retirement Spending

Estimating future expenses is inherently uncertain — life does not stand still (children, housing, health). As a working baseline, start from current spending and scale for inflation.

Example: government pension of €800/month + €1,200/month from the portfolio = **€2,000/month IAPA target**. Identify which expenses are fixed core costs and which are variable using the **Four Pillars** framework.

## Decumulation Instruments

### Distributing ETFs

Distributing ETFs pay out dividends periodically rather than reinvesting them — superficially attractive as a decumulation income source. In practice, several structural flaws make them less suitable than they appear.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| No securities need to be sold — income arrives automatically through dividends | AUM Bias — securities may be selected based on dividend history rather than quality |
| | AUM Liquidation — the fund liquidates holdings to fund payouts, reducing NAV |
| | Distribution Policy — issuers can reduce or suspend dividends at any time |

**AUM Bias** — Distributing ETFs may weight their underlying holdings toward high-dividend securities regardless of their quality. This is a form of factor investing disguised as passive income.

**AUM Liquidation** — Distributing ETFs sell assets from the fund to pay dividends. Upon payment, the ETF's NAV drops by the dividend amount. Mathematically, receiving a dividend from a distributing ETF is equivalent to selling the same value from an accumulating ETF — the total return is the same before taxes. The distributing ETF provides no free income; it returns your own capital.

**Distribution Policy** — Dividend payments are discretionary. In 2020, most companies cut or suspended dividends. A distributing ETF is therefore not a reliable income source in stressed market environments — exactly when reliability matters most.

### Bonds

Bonds as a decumulation income source carry similar vulnerabilities. In 2020, bonds delivered approximately 0% real return, making passive income from a bond allocation effectively impossible for retirees relying on it. Low-rate environments can persist for years, undermining any fixed-income-based decumulation strategy.

## Passive Income

Supplementing investment returns with earned passive income reduces the required withdrawal rate and extends portfolio longevity. Ideas organised by effort:

**Publishing / Content**
- **Verwertungsgesellschaft Wort (VG Wort)** — a German rights collecting society that reimburses authors for online text consumption, based on page views. Relevant for bloggers and content writers.
- **Webinar & Online Courses** — create and sell educational content on platforms such as Udemy
- **Content Creator** — monetised content on YouTube or similar platforms

**Freelance / Consulting**
- **Fiverr** — a freelancer platform for one-shot micro-services; low barrier to entry, highly variable income
