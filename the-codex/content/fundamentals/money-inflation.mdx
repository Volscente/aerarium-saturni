# Money & Inflation

History of money, central banks, and CPI. This page traces the evolution of money from commodity to fiat, examines the role of central banks in controlling the money supply, and explains how inflation is measured and its effects on real returns.

## The Nature of Hard Money

While people are free to use whichever goods they please as media of exchange, history shows that those who use **hard money**—money costly to produce and therefore scarce—benefit the most. Because new supply is negligible, they lose very little value over time. A society that uses hard money directs productive effort toward serving one another rather than toward producing money itself, which adds no value to the economy regardless of its quantity.

## Fiat Currency and the Gold Standard

No pure fiat currency exists in circulation without any form of backing. Contrary to the state theory of money, it was not government that decreed gold as money—rather, governments could only get their money accepted at all by holding gold. The legitimacy of a currency flows from the underlying asset, not from the decree.

## The Digital Money Problem

Any form of electronic payment historically required an intermediary because of the danger of **double-spending**: there was no way to guarantee that a payer was not using the same funds more than once, unless a trusted third party oversaw the account and verified the integrity of each payment. Cash transactions were confined to the physical realm, while all digital payments had to be supervised by a third party.

Third parties are by their nature an added security weakness—introducing new possibilities for theft or technical failure. Payment through intermediaries also leaves parties vulnerable to surveillance and intervention by political authorities.

After years of trial and error, Bitcoin was the first engineering solution to allow digital payments without relying on a trusted third-party intermediary. By being the first digital object that is verifiably scarce, Bitcoin is the first example of digital cash. See [Bitcoin & Cryptocurrency](/instruments/crypto) for a full treatment.

## Government Policy in a Stagnation

When an economy stagnates, governments have two levers to stimulate activity:

### Fiscal Policy

Actions that affect taxation and public spending - for example cutting taxes, increasing government expenditure, or direct transfers to households.

### Monetary Policy

Actions that control the amount of money circulating in the economy. Central banks can:

- **Change interest rates** - lowering rates makes borrowing cheaper, encouraging spending and investment
- **Issue bonds** - buying or selling government bonds on the open market to inject or withdraw liquidity (open market operations)
- **Create money** - expanding the money supply directly (quantitative easing)

## Major International Institutions

Several global bodies influence economic conditions and provide information relevant to long-term investment decisions:

| Institution | Role |
| --- | --- |
| **International Monetary Fund (IMF)** | Advises member countries on economic policy and lends to those facing balance-of-payments crises; aims to increase global GDP stability |
| **World Bank** | Provides financing and expertise to developing countries for infrastructure, education, and poverty reduction |
| **World Trade Organisation (WTO)** | Sets and enforces rules for international trade between member nations |
| **European Central Bank (ECB)** | Sets monetary policy for the Eurozone; controls the euro money supply and sets the reference interest rate |

When inflation rises sharply, central banks typically raise interest rates to slow spending - this slows GDP growth. Monitoring statements from these institutions provides early signals of the macro environment that affects equity and bond valuations.

## Recession

A **recession** is defined as two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth. Recessions are a normal part of the economic cycle and historically have been followed by recovery periods. Long-term investment portfolios are designed to withstand recessions rather than avoid them.

## High Inflation Environment

During periods of persistently high inflation, the real value of cash erodes rapidly. Positioning considerations:

- **Avoid excess liquidity** - cash in a low-yield account loses purchasing power
- **Raw materials (ETCs)** - commodities historically preserve value during inflation
- **REITs** - real assets whose income and valuations tend to track general price levels
- **Non-cyclical equities** - sectors like energy and consumer staples offer partial inflation protection through pricing power
