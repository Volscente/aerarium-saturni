# Commodities

Gold, raw materials, and the case for real assets. Commodities are physical goods - energy, metals, agricultural products - whose prices are driven by supply and demand dynamics distinct from those of equity and bond markets. This page examines when and why to hold them, and the risks involved.

## Role in a Portfolio

Commodities generally have a **low correlation with equities and bonds**, which is their primary value in a portfolio. When financial assets fall, commodity prices often move independently, cushioning the overall drawdown.

This correlation benefit does not apply uniformly across all commodities:

- **Cryptocurrencies** exhibit correlations with global equities that can reach 60% - significantly higher than traditional commodities. They do not provide the same diversification benefit as gold or energy in a downturn.

## Types of Investment

| Type | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Physical commodity | Holding the asset directly (e.g., gold bars). Impractical for most investors |
| Commodity ETC | Exchange-traded commodities - trade like ETFs but backed by the commodity itself (or futures contracts) |
| Commodity futures | Contracts to buy/sell at a future date; complex and subject to roll costs |
| Commodity equities | Stocks of companies in the sector (e.g., miners, oil producers); behave more like equities |

## The Two Types of Investment (Howard Marks)

According to Howard Marks, there are fundamentally only two things you can do with capital:

1. **Buy part of a productive asset** - equities, real estate; assets that generate future cash flows
2. **Lend money** - bonds, deposits; you receive interest in exchange for deferring purchasing power

Commodities - including gold, art, and raw materials - are **non-productive assets**. They do not generate cash flows. Their value depends entirely on what someone else is willing to pay in the future. This is an important distinction when assessing their role in long-term wealth building.

## Historical Performance

Gold returned an average of approximately **2.8% per year between 1980 and 2020** - significantly below equities, bonds, and even inflation in many periods. Its value lies in its low correlation to financial markets, not in its absolute returns.

## Portfolio Guidelines

- Keep commodity exposure **below 10% of total portfolio value**
- Prefer ETCs over physical holdings or futures for most investors
- Gold and "materie prime" (raw materials) can serve as a hedge during periods of **high inflation**
- REIT (Real Estate Investment Trusts) are sometimes grouped with commodities as inflation hedges; they behave similarly to non-cyclical real assets

## High Inflation Considerations

During periods of high inflation:
- Avoid holding excess liquidity - its real value erodes rapidly
- Raw materials (via ETCs) tend to preserve value
- REITs often perform well - real assets appreciate with general price levels
- Non-cyclical equities (e.g., energy, consumer staples) offer partial protection
