# Net Worth

Tracking assets, liabilities, and computation methods. Net worth is the fundamental measure of personal financial health. This page defines how to inventory assets and liabilities accurately and track changes over time.

## Topics

- Definition: net worth = total assets − total liabilities
- Liquid vs. illiquid asset classification
- Valuing real estate, vehicles, and private investments
- Tracking frequency and tooling (spreadsheets, apps)
- The difference between net worth and investable assets
- Bernstein's 4-pillar principles as a framework for personal wealth

## Prerequisites for Investing

Before committing capital to long-term investments, three conditions should be met:

1. **Stable financial situation** — net worth, monthly income statement, and solvency ratio should be understood and tracked
2. **No high-interest debt** — consumer debt (credit cards, personal loans) costs more than any investment is likely to earn
3. **A functioning budget** — income reliably exceeds expenses, with consistent monthly surplus available to deploy

Money held idle in a current account carries an implicit opportunity cost: it could be invested and compounding. This is not an argument for reckless deployment, but a reminder that the decision to *not* invest is itself a financial decision with a measurable cost.

## Four Pillar Money Management

A useful framework for structuring personal finances before and alongside investing. Each pillar has a distinct time horizon and liquidity profile:

| Pillar | Purpose | Horizon | Vehicle |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Day-to-Day** | Operational spending | Immediate | Current account |
| **Emergency Fund** | Unexpected expenses (job loss, medical, repairs) | Access within 1-2 days | High-interest savings account; target 6 months of expenses |
| **Medium-Term** | Planned large expenses (house deposit, car, sabbatical) | 5-10 years | Bonds or money market funds; not equities |
| **Long-Term** | Wealth accumulation and retirement | 10+ years | Diversified equity and bond portfolio |

**Why bonds for the medium-term bucket?** Equities can fall 30-50% and take years to recover. A 5-year horizon is too short to reliably absorb such a drawdown. Bonds and money market funds offer more predictable capital preservation over that window, even if returns are lower.

The long-term bucket is what most of this codex is about. The first three pillars must be funded before meaningful capital is deployed there.

### Emergency Fund: Vehicle Alternatives

When selecting a vehicle for the emergency fund pillar, evaluate on three axes:

- **Cost** — management fees, transaction costs, and broker charges beyond commissions
- **Liquidity** — how quickly can capital be accessed? A 32-day notice period is useless in a genuine emergency
- **Yield** — interest rate or expected return

| Vehicle | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Current account (_conto corrente_) | Instant access; near-zero yield in low-rate environments |
| Notice deposit (_conto deposito_) | Higher yield but withdrawal notice periods vary — verify carefully |
| Money market ETFs | E.g. XEON; low interest rate sensitivity; favourable tax treatment in Italy |
| Short-term government bonds / BOT | Italian Treasury Bills; short maturities |
| Target-maturity bond ETFs (iBonds) | Fixed maturity; more predictable than rolling bond ETFs |

In **low interest rate environments** the yield differences between options collapse toward zero. The current account's liquidity advantage makes it the pragmatic default — yield-chasing adds complexity for negligible gain.

## Net Worth Protection

Three ratios help assess the structural health of a personal balance sheet:

| Ratio | Formula | Target |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Solvency Ratio** | Net Worth ÷ Assets | High — closer to 1 means less leveraged |
| **Equity Ratio** | Liabilities ÷ Net Worth | Low |
| **Debt-to-Assets Ratio** | Liabilities ÷ Assets | Low |

Tracking these ratios over time reveals whether the balance sheet is strengthening (liabilities shrinking relative to assets) or deteriorating.

## Buy vs. Rent

The choice between buying and renting a home is as much a lifestyle decision as a financial one. The common argument that "rent is money thrown away" is incomplete.

**The correct comparison:**

When buying with a mortgage, only the repayment of principal builds equity. The interest paid to the bank is also lost — much like rent. A more accurate frame:

```
Buying cost  = Mortgage interest + Principal repayment + Purchase taxes + Maintenance
Renting cost = Rent paid + Opportunity cost of uninvested down payment
```

These two are more symmetric than commonly assumed. Additional considerations:

- Buying involves purchase taxes and ongoing maintenance costs not borne by renters
- Property may lose value over time — capital appreciation is not guaranteed
- Owning ties up capital that could otherwise compound in a diversified portfolio
- The mortgage interest component is economically equivalent to rent for the duration it is paid
