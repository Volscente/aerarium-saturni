# Selection Rules

Criteria for choosing specific securities. Once an allocation model is chosen, individual instruments must be selected to implement it. This page defines the quantitative and qualitative filters used to evaluate and rank candidates, along with the end-to-end process for building a portfolio.

## Investment Principles

Before selecting any instrument, the right approach to investing should be anchored to these principles:

- **Diversification** — no single asset should dominate the portfolio
- **Low fees** — every basis point of TER compounds against you over decades
- **Tax efficiency** — long-term holdings typically receive preferential tax treatment; minimise taxable events
- **Long-term horizon** — invest to own a market index, not to rent it for short-term gains
- Read disclosures; if anything is unclear, ask the fund manager before investing

## Analysis: Fundamentals vs. Technicals

| | Fundamental Analysis | Technical Analysis |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Focus | Financial health of the underlying company or asset | Price charts and historical price patterns |
| Inputs | Earnings, competitors, interest rates, macroeconomics, regulation | Moving averages, support/resistance levels, volume |
| Time horizon | Long-term | Short-term |
| Appropriate for | Long-term investors | Active traders |

For long-term investors, fundamental analysis is the relevant framework. Technical analysis attempts to predict future price movements from past prices — a premise that is difficult to reconcile with the Efficient Market Hypothesis.

**Fundamental analysis** aims to determine a stock's intrinsic value from assets, expected earnings growth, dividends, interest rates, and risk. Its practitioners include value investors who buy when market price is below intrinsic value and sell when it converges.

**Technical analysis** interprets past price patterns to forecast future movements. It is associated with the view that price trends persist long enough to exploit — a claim the Random Walk hypothesis directly challenges.

### Limitations of Security Analysis

Both forms of analysis face structural limits on how reliable their outputs can be:

- **Random events** — a large share of company outcomes is driven by genuinely unforeseeable developments
- **Earnings quality** — reported earnings can be distorted by accounting discretion or fraud; analysts often work from the same flawed inputs
- **Analyst error** — models are simplifications; even skilled analysts make systematic mistakes
- **Talent attrition** — the best analysts tend to move into portfolio management or sales roles, reducing the average quality of published research
- **Conflicts of interest** — analysts at investment banks face institutional pressure to avoid negative ratings on client companies

Even Benjamin Graham — widely regarded as the father of fundamental security analysis — ultimately concluded that fundamental analysis could no longer be counted on to produce superior investment returns consistently, and doubted whether the effort justifies its cost. The implication for long-term investors is clear: broad, low-cost index exposure typically outperforms active stock-picking over sufficiently long horizons.

## ETF Selection Checklist

| Criterion | Threshold / Guidance |
| --- | --- |
| TER / Expense Ratio | ≤ 0.2% good; ≥ 0.5% red flag |
| Tracking error | As low as possible; compare ETF vs. benchmark returns |
| Index replication | Physical replication preferred over synthetic |
| Fund domicile | Ireland or Luxembourg (UCITS) preferred for WHT treaties |
| AUM | Larger is better; more liquidity and more reliable tracking |
| Share class | Accumulating preferred for long-term tax efficiency |
| Currency hedging | Avoid — hedging is expensive and erodes returns |
| Fund age | Minimum 1-2 years of live history |
| Benchmark comparison | Consistent ETF outperformance of its own index may signal replication risk |
| Holdings concentration | No single holding > 5-7% of fund weight |

Research tools: [JustETF.com](https://justetf.com), [etfdb.com](https://etfdb.com), issuer websites.

## Rebalancing

Rebalancing restores the portfolio to its target allocation after market movements cause weights to drift. Key decisions:

- **Frequency**: annually is a sensible default; triggered rebalancing (when any weight drifts by more than a threshold, e.g., 5%) is an alternative
- **Method**: prefer adding new capital to underweight positions rather than selling overweight ones — avoids triggering capital gains
- **What to rebalance**: equities/bonds split, geographic exposure, sector exposure
- **Caution**: over-rebalancing generates unnecessary tax events and transaction costs; once every 1-3 years is sufficient for most portfolios

## Portfolio Building Process

A structured process for going from a blank sheet to a running portfolio:

**Step 1 — Establish financial prerequisites**

Before investing, ensure:
- Net worth, income statement, and solvency ratio are understood and tracked
- An **emergency fund** covering 6 months of expenses is in place (accessible in 1-2 days, ideally in a savings account earning interest)
- Medium-term expenses (house, car, 5-10 year horizon) are covered in bonds or money market funds — not equities

**Step 2 — Define risk tolerance**

Determine the equities/bonds split based on age, income stability, and psychological tolerance for drawdowns. Use the `100 − Age` heuristic as a starting point.

**Step 3 — Identify target indices (optional)**

Start from a global world index (e.g., MSCI ACWI) as the core. Add specific regional or sector ETFs to tilt toward areas you want to overweight. Alternatively, start directly from ETF characteristics (region, asset class, sector).

**Step 4 — Research model portfolios (optional)**

Browse [portfoliocharts.com](https://portfoliocharts.com/portfolios/) or [lazyportfolioetf.com](https://www.lazyportfolioetf.com/) for established model allocations. Backtest candidates at [portfoliovisualizer.com](https://www.portfoliovisualizer.com/) using SPY as the benchmark.

Compare: Standard Deviation, Sharpe Ratio, CAGR. Vary the start date to ensure performance is not start-date dependent.

**Step 5 — Asset class allocation**

Decide the percentage split: equities, bonds, and optionally commodities or REITs. Avoid cryptocurrencies and thematic ETFs.

**Step 6 — Security selection**

Use [JustETF.com](https://justetf.com) to filter ETFs:
1. Asset class (equities, bonds, commodities)
2. Region (World, EU, US, Emerging Markets)
3. Currency denomination
4. Fund domicile
5. Use of profits (Accumulating preferred)
6. Fund size
7. Sort by TER (ascending)

Compare shortlisted ETFs side-by-side using the "Compare Selection in Details" feature. Check TER, volatility, fund size, and multi-year return history.

A single "World All-Cap Equity" ETF + a "World Bonds" ETF already provides a well-diversified two-fund portfolio.

**Step 7 — Active diversification (optional)**

If adding sector or regional tilts, use [etfdb.com](https://etfdb.com) or [etf.com](https://etf.com) to screen additional candidates. Validate the enhanced portfolio on Portfolio Visualizer.

**Step 8 — Execute and invest continuously**

Invest your target amount each month, regardless of market conditions. Do not time the market. Studies show consistent monthly investment outperforms waiting for dips — you can never reliably identify the bottom.

**Step 9 — Rebalance**

Every year (or when weights drift significantly):
- Restore equities/bonds ratio
- Restore sector and regional weights
- Shift the portfolio incrementally more conservative as you age

**Step 10 — The Boring Middle**

Once the portfolio is set up, let it run. The vast majority of issues resolve themselves over a sufficiently long horizon. Resist the urge to react to market news.
